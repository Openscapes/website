What we do

The scope of environmental science spans vastly different ecosystems, scales, and organisms. But no matter what our research questions are, scientists are united by data. We all need to analyze data, and do so in a way that is efficient, reproducible, and easily communicated. At Openscapes, we champion open practices in environmental science so we can uncover data-driven solutions faster. Here’s how:



Engage

We build awareness and excitement. We engage scientists with empathy, art, and storytelling, using narratives like our own path to better science in less time.

Empower

We help build confidence and skills. We empower scientists by understanding how they work and connecting them with existing open software and communities so they can develop the skills they need.

Amplify

We build champions and communities. We amplify scientists’ efforts through academic seminars as well as through media, blogs, and social media to increase the visibility of open practices on campus and online.

Champions Program

The Champions program provides mentorship for early career researchers in leadership roles (e.g. faculty, lecturers, program managers). Because formal computing and data training are often not part of environmental education, it is vital that emerging scientific leaders have the opportunity to engage with existing tools, practices, and communities. The Champions program builds awareness of efficient and collaborative practices and helps Champions’ labs take the next steps appropriate for them. Openscapes helps scientists become visible Champions for open practices and data science, and promote them through their labs, teaching, and departments.

Openscapes Ethos

Openscapes is about opening the landscapes of science, which means the full spectrum of openness. It means open science: open data, open knowledge, and using the internet for good. But, importantly, it also means open culture: collaboration, empathy, and kindness. It is about creating the positive culture we want in science, so we can do better science in less time.

Start a coding club

By Julie Lowndes on November 16, 2018

How to start a coding club Summary: start small, be hands-on, use existing tutorials, have fun. Here is my advice for starting a coding club. An example is Eco-Data-Science at UCSB. We are currently a community of >100 people; we meet 2x/month at the UCSB Library’s Collaboratory and skill share peer-to-peer, with all resources organized and archived on the website. But we started off small, with co-founder Jamie giving tutorials to friends one-on-one.

About us

Openscapes is for environmental scientists by environmental scientists with the vision that we can get to environmental solutions faster if we are more open and collaborative with how we do science. Openscapes is operated by the National Center for Ecological Analysis & Synthesis (NCEAS) and is being incubated by a Mozilla Fellowship awarded to Julia Stewart Lowndes.
